03 —Why repair gets expensive

Old units don't
fail cheaply.

The bigger problem isn't only cost — it's uncertainty. A $2,400 repair that lasts six months isn't a repair; it's a deposit on the next failure.

Aging compressors

The most expensive single part. Once one starts cycling abnormally, the clock is on the others.

Typical repair · $2,200–4,500

Refrigerant leaks

Coil leaks on older units can be hard to isolate, and legacy refrigerants (R-22, R-410A) are increasingly costly or restricted.

Diagnose + recharge · $900–2,800

Obsolete parts

Boards, valves, and proprietary fittings for 2005-era units can take weeks to source — if they're still made at all.

Wait time · 2–6 weeks

Bench work & removal

Many serious repairs need the unit pulled from the ceiling or closet. Once you're paying for that twice, replacement gets cheap.

Removal + reinstall · $600–1,200

05 —Warning signs

Six signs your unit
may need attention.

If you're seeing two or more of these, it's time for a Health Check — before the unit decides for you.

— 01

Unusual noises or cycling

New rattles, clunks, or the unit short-cycling on and off without reaching setpoint.

— 02

Service calls in the past 18 months

One service call is maintenance. Two or more is the unit telling you something.

— 03

Building is 10+ years old

If the building is from 2016 or earlier and the unit is original, it's worth understanding its condition and replacement timeline.

— 04

Loss of cooling or heating capacity

Rooms don't get as cool or warm as they used to — often the first sign of a slow refrigerant leak.

— 05

Visible water or condensate

Pooling, drips, or stains around the unit can point to condensate problems that cause floods and affect insurance premiums.

— 06

Strata fees rising for repairs

Building heat-pump maintenance costs climbing year over year — your unit may not be far behind.

08 —Frequently asked

Real questions from condo owners.

If yours isn't here, call us. We're happy to talk through your specific situation before you book anything.

— 01 Is my heat pump my responsibility, or the strata's? +

In most BC strata buildings, the in-suite heat pump is the owner's responsibility, while shared building mechanical systems are the strata's. But every building's bylaws and Form B are different — we'll help you read yours during the Health Check, and contact your strata or property manager if needed.

— 02 How much does a typical condo heat pump replacement cost? +

For a like-for-like in-suite water-source heat pump in a Lower Mainland condo, replacement typically runs $8,900–$14,500 fully installed, depending on tonnage, location in the suite, and access. Group pricing (3+ owners in one building) reduces per-unit cost meaningfully. We provide written, fixed-price quotes — no change-order surprises.

— 03 How long does the replacement take? +

A single in-suite replacement is typically one working day. We arrive in the morning, remove the old unit, install the new one, commission it, and the suite has heat and cooling back by end of day. We bring matting and floor protection — your floors and walls stay clean.

— 04 What's included in the Condo Heat Pump Health Check? +

A full on-site inspection: refrigerant pressures and temperatures, electrical readings, fan and blower performance, condensate pan and drain, building water-side performance, age and serial verification, and a visual check of coils, compressor housing, and controls. You receive a written report with photographs, a condition rating, and our honest recommendation on repair, monitor, or replace.

— 05 Do you handle the strata / building paperwork? +

Yes. We provide insurance certificates, WorkSafeBC documentation, a written scope-of-work for your strata Form K, and direct communication with your property manager or strata council if needed. For group pricing across multiple owners, we send a single strata-facing scope and coordinate scheduling around bylaws (elevator bookings, work-hour restrictions, etc.).

— 06 Is financing available? +

Financing is available for qualified customers, OAC. Many condo owners also plan replacement through existing home equity, lines of credit, or personal financing — we don't push HVAC-specific financing. Ask during the quote and we'll outline the options.

— 07 My quote from another contractor seems high — will you really review it? +

Yes — that's exactly what the Second Opinion offer is for. Send us the quote (photo or PDF is fine), give us 30 minutes, and we'll tell you whether it looks fair, what's missing, and what an alternate scope or brand might cost. No obligation, no pressure to switch.
